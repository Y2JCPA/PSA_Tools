## Current Notes (what's on the page now)

1. **Traditional IRA deductions** may not be deductible against Israeli income. Israel does not recognize US IRA contributions as a deduction from Israeli-source income.

2. **Roth IRA is NOT recognized as tax-exempt by Israel.** Growth inside a Roth IRA is taxable in Israel as it accrues, or upon distribution, depending on your reporting position.

3. **Israeli retirement accounts** (keren pensia, keren hishtalmut, kupat gemel) are NOT US retirement accounts and do not count toward IRS contribution limits.

4. **Early withdrawal penalty:** 401(k) or IRA withdrawals before age 59½ generally incur a 10% US penalty plus ordinary income tax.

5. **Treaty Article 17** of the US-Israel tax treaty may provide some relief for pension distributions. Consult your advisor for applicability.

6. **Backdoor Roth conversions** work for US federal tax purposes, but Israel may treat the conversion as a taxable event. Coordinate with both US and Israeli tax advisors.

## Suggested additions / things I'm unsure about (Yaacov please review)

7. **Foreign employer = no US 401(k).** If your employer is an Israeli company (even with a US payroll agent), they almost certainly do not offer a US-qualified 401(k) or 403(b) plan. Your retirement savings options are limited to IRAs (Traditional/Roth), and possibly a Solo 401(k) if you have qualifying self-employment income.

8. **Solo 401(k) requires SE income.** W-2 wages from a foreign employer do NOT qualify as self-employment income for Solo 401(k) purposes. You need actual Schedule C / Schedule SE income to make employee deferrals to a Solo 401(k).

9. **Israeli pension contributions and the treaty.** Under the US-Israel tax treaty (Article 17/23), there may be arguments for deducting Israeli mandatory pension contributions on your US return, but this is a grey area and aggressive. Most practitioners do not take this position.

10. **Keren Hishtalmut** — employer contributions may be excludable from US income under certain treaty positions, but withdrawals may be taxable. The treatment is complex and depends on timing, amounts, and whether you take the position that it's a pension or compensation.

11. **PFIC issues in Israeli retirement accounts.** Israeli pension funds, gemel funds, and hishtalmut often invest in Israeli mutual funds that are PFICs for US purposes. However, the accounts themselves may be exempt from PFIC reporting under certain treaty/regulatory positions. This is actively debated among practitioners.

12. **Roth IRA and Israeli capital gains tax.** Israel taxes Roth IRA gains either annually (mark-to-market) or at distribution — there is no settled position. Some practitioners report annually, others at distribution. Either way, you're paying Israeli tax on what the US considers tax-free growth.

13. **Contribution timing for olim.** New olim in their first year of Aliyah may have a split year (US resident part-year, Israeli resident part-year). IRA contributions require US earned income — make sure contributions are supported by US-source earned income, not just Israeli salary.
